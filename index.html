Overview

Can a value function trained across many robots act as a reusable design model? Classical robot co-design pairs an outer search over embodiments with an inner control loop, requiring a fresh reinforcement learning run for every new robot or design space. We instead train a single embodiment-aware policy and value function over a wide distribution of robot designs. Once trained, the value function is frozen and reused as a differentiable surrogate: we co-design new robots through value gradients, optimizing up to 1100 continuous embodiment parameters per robot without any new RL training.

Value-Gradient Design Search

During training, each parallel environment samples a new robot design and keeps it fixed for the episode. A URMA-based policy and ensemble critic learn to control and evaluate the full distribution of designs. We modify the standard URMA critic into a direct-design critic that makes value predictions more sensitive to the embodiment parameters, yielding stronger design gradients.

After training, the policy and critic are frozen. Value-Gradient Design Search (VGDS) optimizes a normalized design vector by gradient ascent on the average critic prediction over a fixed bank of states, regularized by a soft trust region around a reference design. Each design step is the gradient of the frozen critic w.r.t. the embodiment parameters, plus the analytic trust-region term — clipped per-parameter and projected back into the valid design space. The result is a deterministic, batched, gradient-based co-design loop that reuses one trained model across many target robots.

Multi-Embodiment Training Set

Our largest training set spans 50 robots — 15 quadrupeds, 31 bipeds and humanoids, and 4 hexapods. A single URMA policy and critic are trained across all of them, with continuous embodiment randomization of masses, inertias, geometry, joint limits, PD gains, and actuator properties. Design dimensions per robot range from 190 up to 1177 continuous parameters.

The Unitree Go2 quadruped, MIT Humanoid, and Golem hexapod (highlighted in red) are the main target robots for our co-design experiments and are held out from morphology-class training sets to test cross-embodiment generalization.

Single-Robot Co-Design

In the simplest setting, we train a policy and critic on randomized versions of a single robot and use them to recover better designs from perturbed embodiments. Across the Go2, MIT Humanoid, and Golem, VGDS matches or beats a broad suite of derivative-free baselines — Bayesian Optimization, CMA-ES, PSO, CEM, DE, ARS, TuRBO, and the gradient-based GC-PFO — all sharing the same frozen critic as their objective. Compared to RL-based co-design baselines that train a new policy per initial design (Schaff2019, FEACRL, Transform2Act, BodyGen, Stackelberg PPO), VGDS reaches on-par or slightly better return while co-designing each additional robot in ~1–2 minutes after the one-time training run.

Cross-Embodiment Generalization

We then train policies and critics on multiple base robots per morphology class — with the target robot held out — and on the full 50-robot training set. When trained on all 50 robots, VGDS finds designs that exceed the nominal URDF for the Go2 and MIT Humanoid. Generalization is strongest where the training distribution is most diverse, showing that a multi-embodiment critic carries information from the broader robot distribution into co-design of unseen robots.

Analyzing Discovered Designs

Beyond optimizing full embodiments, the same value gradients can identify performance-limiting design and control parameters. Grouping the final design update by physical parameter type — actuator torque and velocity limits, body masses and inertias, geometry, PD gains, action scaling — gives a local diagnosis of what the critic would change to improve a given robot. This turns the multi-embodiment value function into a reusable design analysis tool for both new robots and existing platforms.
